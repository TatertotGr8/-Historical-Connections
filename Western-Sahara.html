Sands of Discord: 
Tracing the Roots of the Western Sahara Conflict

The Western Sahara conflict is a dispute over who has control over the territory of Western Sahara in North Africa. The conflict began when the Spanish colonized the area in the late 1800s, and it continued after many African countries gained independence in the mid-1900s. In 1975, the Madrid Accord ended Spanish colonial rule but also contributed to the ongoing conflict. This project aims to study the historical connections between Spanish colonization, decolonization movements, the Madrid Accord, the Green March, and the involvement of the United Nations in the Western Sahara conflict. By looking at how the conflict has evolved, the project hopes to understand the underlying causes that have led to the conflict lasting for so long. The project will also analyze the conflict's current political, economic, and humanitarian implications. By doing this, the project hopes to increase understanding of the complex dynamics involved in the Western Sahara conflict and contribute to efforts to find a solution that will last.

Spanish Colonization 

During the Colonial Era, Western Sahara was colonized by Spain. This period began in 1884 after the Berlin Conference, where European powers divided Africa among themselves, and Spain claimed Western Sahara as a colony. Spain established a colonial administration in Western Sahara and exploited its resources, particularly phosphate deposits, and fisheries.
The Sahrawi people, indigenous to Western Sahara, resisted Spanish rule through various uprisings and protests. In 1957, the pro-independence group Polisario Front was formed, advocating for self-determination and independence for Western Sahara. The Sahrawi people sought to reclaim their land and exercise their right to self-governance. The Spanish colonization of Western Sahara lasted for several decades, during which the Sahrawi people experienced political, social, and economic changes due to Spanish rule. However, tensions between the Sahrawi people and the Spanish colonial administration and the broader context of decolonization movements across Africa eventually led to the end of Spanish colonization in Western Sahara in the mid-1970s.

The impact of Spanish colonization on the present conflict in Western Sahara can be seen through the enduring disputes over sovereignty. When Spain withdrew from the region in 1975, it left behind a power vacuum that Morocco and Mauritania sought to fill. Morocco claimed historical and cultural ties to Western Sahara, asserting its right to control the territory. However, this was met with resistance from the Sahrawi people, who formed the Polisario Front to fight for self-determination and independence. The ripple effects of Spanish colonization are evident in the ongoing struggle between Morocco, the Polisario Front, and the international community, which has been involved in mediation efforts to resolve the conflict.

To further understand the socioeconomic ramifications, examining the exploitation of natural resources during Spanish colonization is crucial. The region's abundant fisheries were heavily exploited by Spanish powers, leading to overfishing and a decline in fish stocks. This not only had ecological consequences but also impacted the livelihoods of the local Sahrawi population, who depended on fishing for sustenance and income. The disruption of the marine ecosystem has persisted beyond the colonial era, affecting the present-day fishing industry in Western Sahara.

Additionally, Spanish companies extracted phosphates, a valuable mineral used in fertilizers, from Western Sahara during colonization. This resource extraction led to land degradation, pollution, and the displacement of indigenous communities. The environmental degradation caused by phosphate mining continues to challenge the region's sustainability and development. The struggle for control over Western Sahara's natural resources, including fisheries and phosphates, has further complicated the conflict and contributed to ongoing tensions.

The lasting effects of Spanish colonization are not limited to political and socioeconomic aspects but also encompass human rights issues. During the colonial period, the Spanish authorities implemented policies that led to fragmentation and division among the Sahrawi population. This included the forced resettlement of Sahrawi communities, suppression of indigenous cultures and languages, and the imposition of Western legal systems. These actions have impacted the Sahrawi people's identity, social cohesion, and political aspirations. The violation of human rights during colonization has continued in different forms during the conflict, with reports of human rights abuses, forced displacement, and restrictions on freedom of expression and association.

In the post-colonial era, resource exploitation and depletion have persisted in Western Sahara, particularly under Moroccan control. The Moroccan government has actively engaged in the exploitation of the region's resources, disregarding the rights and aspirations of the Sahrawi people. One notable example is the fishing industry. Moroccan companies, often in collaboration with international partners, have engaged in extensive fishing activities off the coast of Western Sahara. This has led to the depletion of fish stocks and has had a detrimental impact on the livelihoods of local fishermen, who rely on these resources for sustenance and income.

Mining phosphates is another significant aspect of resource exploitation in the Western Sahara. Phosphate reserves in the region are among the largest in the world, making them highly valuable. The Moroccan government has granted concessions to foreign companies, allowing them to extract phosphates from Western Sahara. These activities have been widely criticized for their lack of consideration for the rights and interests of the Sahrawi people. The economic benefits derived from the resource exploitation in Western Sahara primarily benefit Morocco and the companies involved rather than the local population. The Sahrawi people, who have long sought self-determination and control over their resources, are largely excluded from decision-making processes and do not reap the benefits of the economic activities in their territory. This further perpetuates inequalities and grievances within the region.

International organizations and human rights groups have raised concerns about the ongoing resource exploitation in Western Sahara. Through its peacekeeping mission in the region (MINURSO), the United Nations has highlighted the need for a fair and transparent management of the region's resources, ensuring that the interests and rights of the Sahrawi people are respected. However, challenges remain in enforcing these principles and holding those involved in the exploitation accountable. The persistence of resource exploitation and depletion in Western Sahara is a tangible reminder of the lasting impacts of Spanish colonization. It underscores the need for a comprehensive resolution to the Western Saharan conflict addressing historical injustices. Reports indicate that individuals are arrested without warrants, held incommunicado, and subjected to ill-treatment and torture during detention. These actions aim to suppress dissent and stifle the Sahrawi people's voices. Freedom of expression and assembly are also severely restricted in Western Sahara. Sahrawi activists, journalists, and human rights defenders face censorship, harassment, and intimidation for voicing their opinions or documenting human rights abuses. Such restrictions impede the Sahrawi people's ability to exercise their fundamental rights and participate in shaping their future. Western Sahara War

The Western Sahara War was a conflict that took place between 1975 and 1991. It erupted following the Madrid Accords, which divided Western Sahara between Morocco and Mauritania after Spain's withdrawal from the region. The indigenous Sahrawi population, represented by the Polisario Front, opposed this division and launched an armed struggle against Moroccan and Mauritanian forces.

The war was characterized by guerrilla warfare and military engagements, with the Polisario Front aiming to establish an independent state in Western Sahara. They fought against Moroccan and Mauritanian forces to regain control over the entire territory. The conflict lasted over a decade, resulting in significant casualties and displacements. It also led to establishment of Sahrawi refugee camps in Algeria, where many displaced Sahrawis sought shelter.

In 1991, a UN-brokered ceasefire was agreed upon, effectively ending the active phase of the conflict. The ceasefire established a buffer zone and paved the way for deploying the United Nations Mission for the Referendum in Western Sahara (MINURSO). Despite the ceasefire, the underlying issue of Western Sahara's political status and self-determination remained unresolved. The promised referendum for the Sahrawi people to decide their fate has yet to occur due to disagreements over the voter eligibility criteria and the question of independence versus integration with Morocco.

The Western Sahara War profoundly impacted the region, leading to ongoing diplomatic efforts and international involvement in seeking a peaceful resolution to the conflict. The situation remains complex, with Morocco maintaining de facto control over most of Western Sahara and the Polisario Front continuing to advocate for self-determination and independence.

The Sahrawi refugee crisis is a pressing humanitarian issue resulting from the Western Sahara War. Following the outbreak of the conflict, many Sahrawis fled their homes and sought refuge in neighboring Algeria. Today, thousands of Sahrawi refugees remain in camps, such as the Tindouf camps, enduring protracted displacement and living in challenging conditions.

These camps, established decades ago, were initially intended as temporary settlements. However, the prolonged nature of the conflict and the failure to resolve it has resulted in a protracted refugee situation. Generations have grown up in these camps with limited access to essential services, education, and economic opportunities. The refugees in the camps face numerous challenges. Limited resources and infrastructure strain the provision of essential services, such as healthcare, education, and clean water. Economic opportunities are scarce, leading to high unemployment rates and dependence on external aid. The lack of durable solutions and the uncertain future contribute to a sense of hopelessness and frustration among the refugee population. The remote location of the camps in the inhospitable desert environment poses additional challenges. Harsh climatic conditions, including extreme heat and limited access to water sources, exacerbate the difficulties faced by the refugees.

The Sahrawi refugee crisis requires ongoing attention and support from the international community. Humanitarian organizations and donor countries are crucial in providing humanitarian assistance, including food, shelter, healthcare, and education. Spice Themes

The historical SPICE themes refer to five broad categories that are commonly used to analyze and understand the development and evolution of human societies over time. These themes are Social, Political, Interactions With Humans and the Environment, Culture, ​Economic. Scholars and researchers use these themes to organize their thinking and research, and to help identify the key factors that have influenced the development of societies.

  • Social : The social theme can be used to analyze how societies have structured social relationships, and how social structures have developed over time. This can include studying the roles and expectations of individuals based on factors such as gender, race, class, and other social categories, as well as the ways in which social structures have influenced political and economic systems.​ 
  • Political : ​The political theme can be used to analyze how societies have organized themselves politically, and how political systems have developed over time. This can include studying the development of different forms of government, the distribution of power, and the relationships between rulers and subjects.
  • Interactions With Humans and the Environment:  The environmental theme can be used to analyze how societies have interacted with their natural surroundings, and how environmental factors have influenced social and political structures. This can include studying the impact of climate, geography, and natural resources on the development of societies. 
  • Culture : The cultural theme can be used to analyze how societies have developed unique beliefs, values, customs, and practices, and how these cultural elements have influenced social and political structures. This can include studying the development of language, religion, art, music, literature, and social norms. 
  • Economic : The economic theme can be used to analyze how societies have organized their economies and how economic systems have developed over time. This can include studying the development of agriculture, trade, manufacturing, and finance, as well as the impact of economic systems on social and political structures.
