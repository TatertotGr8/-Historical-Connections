Shadow of Brutality:
The Roots of Chile's Political Turmoil

This historical project seeks to investigate Chile's political turmoil and conflict following the election of socialist Salvador Allende as president in 1970, his subsequent overthrow by General Augusto Pinochet's coup in 1973, and the resulting violence and human rights abuses under Pinochet's regime. The project will center on pivotal events such as the National Stadium tragedy and General Pinochet's arrest and imprisonment. The project will draw on both primary and secondary sources, including government documents, news reports, personal testimonies, books, academic articles, and documentaries, to provide a comprehensive understanding of the historical context and significance of these events, as well as their long-term impact on Chilean society and politics. The research will also investigate the complexity of Chilean history throughout the 1970s, investigating the links between Allende and Pinochet, as well as their opposing political views. Despite their disagreements, the two leaders had served in the military together, with Pinochet serving as Allende's army chief. The study hopes to provide insight on the complex political and social factors that molded Chile throughout this turbulent time in its history by investigating these links. The research will also investigate how events from the 1970s continue to impact Chilean society and politics today.

Salvador Allende Wins Chile Presidential Election 

Salvador Allende won the 1970 Chilean presidential election as the candidate of the socialist coalition, making him the first Marxist to be elected as president of a Latin American country through democratic means. Allende's presidency was marked by efforts to nationalize key industries and implement socialist reforms, leading to opposition from right-wing groups and a military coup in 1973.

The economic and political changes initiated by Allende's government, including the nationalization of industries and land reforms, caused significant opposition from the political right and business community. Allegations of corruption and mismanagement within the Allende government further fueled the opposition. The tensions eventually led to a military coup led by General Augusto Pinochet, who overthrew Allende's government and established a brutal dictatorship lasting over 17 years.

Authoritarianism is a form of governance characterized by the concentration of power and authority in the hands of a single leader, a small group of individuals, or an autocratic regime. In an authoritarian system, the ruling entity exercises absolute control over all aspects of society, including political, economic, and social spheres. This form of government disregards individual liberties, limits or eliminates political opposition, and suppresses dissenting voices. At the core of authoritarianism lies the belief in a strong central authority that wields unchecked power, often justified by claims of maintaining social stability, national security, or economic development. The leader or ruling entity tends to hold and exert power without meaningful accountability, frequently employing censorship, propaganda, surveillance, and intimidation methods to maintain control. 

The belief in authoritarian rule and the concentration of power was central to General Augusto Pinochet's regime in Chile. Pinochet and his supporters viewed a robust and centralized leadership as essential for maintaining order, stability, and national security after the political and economic turmoil preceding his rise to power.

Pinochet justified his authoritarian rule by portraying himself as a strong and decisive leader who could rescue Chile from what he perceived as a state of crisis. He presented his regime as a necessary response to the perceived threats of communism and leftist ideologies, which he believed endangered Chilean society's social fabric and stability. This belief system enabled Pinochet to consolidate power in his own hands, along with a close circle of military officers, and to institute a regime characterized by repression and control.

Under Pinochet's rule, human rights abuses were widespread, with political opponents, activists, and ordinary citizens targeted for imprisonment, torture, and execution. The belief in authoritarianism and the concentration of power justified these grave human rights violations, as dissent and opposition were viewed as threats to the regime's stability and security. The regime's security forces, such as the notorious National Intelligence Directorate (DINA), operated with impunity and employed brutal tactics to suppress dissent and maintain control.

Censorship and the suppression of political dissent were also integral components of Pinochet's authoritarian rule. The belief system propagated by the regime deemed it necessary to control information flow and manipulate public discourse to maintain the regime's legitimacy and control over society. Media outlets were heavily censored, dissenting voices were silenced, and opposition political parties and organizations were banned or marginalized. Intellectuals, artists, and academics who expressed critical views or challenged the regime's narrative were often persecuted or forced into exile.

The belief in authoritarian rule also established a highly centralized and repressive state apparatus. Pinochet's regime sought to dismantle democratic institutions, including the Congress and political parties, and replace them with a tightly controlled system. The regime's Constitution of 1980, which was drafted under military supervision and without popular participation, concentrated power in the executive branch and limited democratic checks and balances. This concentration of power facilitated Pinochet's control over the country's political, economic, and social spheres.

It is important to note that the belief in authoritarian rule and the concentration of power was not universally accepted or supported within Chile. Opposition to Pinochet's regime emerged from various sectors of society, including human rights activists, student movements, labor unions, and progressive political parties. These individuals and groups challenged the regime's belief system, advocating for democracy, human rights, and social justice.

The legacy of Pinochet's authoritarian rule continues to shape Chilean society and politics. The memory of the human rights abuses, censorship, and political repression under the regime remains contentious, with ongoing efforts to seek truth, justice, and accountability. The belief system that justified authoritarianism during Pinochet's rule serves as a reminder of the importance of safeguarding democratic principles, protecting human rights, and challenging systems that concentrate power and suppress dissent. 

Neoliberalism is an economic and political ideology that emerged in the latter half of the 20th century, gaining prominence in the 1980s and continuing to shape global policies today. Rooted in classical liberal principles, neoliberalism advocates for limited government intervention in the economy, free trade, deregulation, privatization, and promoting individual liberty and market forces as drivers of economic growth. At its core, neoliberalism emphasizes the importance of the free market as an efficient mechanism for allocating resources and determining prices. It argues that when left to its own devices, the market can lead to optimal outcomes, generating prosperity, innovation, and overall societal well-being. Neoliberal thinkers contend that reducing government regulations and allowing market forces to operate freely will foster competition, encourage entrepreneurship, and stimulate economic growth.

Under General Augusto Pinochet's rule, the belief in neoliberalism played a central role in shaping Chile's economic policies and profoundly impacted the country's economic landscape. Neoliberalism, an ideology rooted in free-market principles, advocates for privatizing state-owned enterprises, deregulation, fiscal discipline, and limited government intervention in the economy. Pinochet's regime embraced these neoliberal principles and implemented economic reforms known as the "Chicago Boys" policies. These policies were influenced by economists who had studied at the University of Chicago and advocated for market-oriented reforms. The goal was to transform Chile's economy from a state-controlled and protectionist system to one that promoted private enterprise and free-market competition.

One of the critical elements of Pinochet's economic reforms was the privatization of state-owned enterprises. Under this policy, many industries and services that were previously owned and operated by the state, such as telecommunications, utilities, and mining, were sold to private investors. This privatization aimed to introduce competition, increase efficiency, and attract foreign investment.

Deregulation was another critical aspect of Pinochet's neoliberal economic agenda. The regime dismantled numerous regulations and restrictions on businesses, aiming to create a more flexible and market-driven economy. This included reducing barriers to trade and investment, simplifying administrative processes, and loosening labor regulations.

Pinochet's government also pursued fiscal discipline by implementing austerity measures to reduce public spending and address the country's fiscal imbalances. This involved cutting social welfare programs, subsidies, and public services, reducing government expenditure. Additionally, the regime pursued a financial liberalization policy, opening up Chile's financial sector to international capital flows and facilitating the entry of foreign banks and financial institutions.

The neoliberal reforms implemented under Pinochet's rule significantly impacted Chile's economy. While some argue that these reforms contributed to economic growth and modernization, others highlight the emerging social costs and inequalities. Proponents of neoliberalism point to expanding the private sector, increasing foreign investment, and improving business climate indicators of success. However, critics argue that the neoliberal policies led to income disparities, social inequality, and the concentration of wealth in the hands of a few. The reduction in social welfare programs and the emphasis on market forces contributed to increased poverty rates and decreased social protections. The neoliberal economic model implemented under Pinochet's rule has been the subject of ongoing debate in Chile, with differing perspectives on its long-term effects on the country's socio-economic development.

It emerged as a significant force during the 20th century, particularly during the Cold War, when global tensions between the capitalist West and the communist East peaked. At its essence, communism advocates for establishing a classless society where the means of production are owned and controlled by the community. It aims to eliminate private property, create economic equality, and establish a system of government in which the state represents the interests of the working class. However, anti-Communism challenges these ideas and raises concerns about the implementation and consequences of such a system.

Pinochet's anti-communist stance was rooted in the broader context of the Cold War, during which the United States and its allies sought to contain the spread of communism worldwide. Pinochet positioned himself and his regime as staunch anti-communists, aligning Chile with Western powers and presenting the regime's actions as part of a global struggle against the communist threat.

Under the banner of anti-communism, Pinochet's regime engaged in severe repression and human rights abuses targeting perceived political opponents and individuals associated with left-wing groups. This included widespread arrests, imprisonments, disappearances, torture, and extrajudicial killings. The regime justified these actions as necessary to protect Chile from the perceived danger of communism and ensure the nation's stability and security.

The belief in anti-communism served as a powerful tool of justification and propaganda for the regime. Pinochet and his supporters framed their actions as protecting Chilean society from the perceived chaos and upheaval that they associated with communist ideologies. It is essential to note that the regime's definition of communism was often broad and encompassed not only armed guerrilla groups but also political opponents, labor unions, intellectuals, and activists who advocated for social justice and progressive change. This broad interpretation of communism allowed the regime to target a wide range of individuals and groups that it considered threats to its power and ideology.

The regime sought to dismantle leftist organizations, silence critical voices, and reshape the country's political and social structures to align with its uncompromising vision. While Pinochet and his core supporters embraced the anti-communist belief system, it was not universally accepted or supported within Chile. There were individuals, groups, and sectors of society that opposed the regime's actions and sought to challenge its ideology. The term "Cult of Personality" refers to a phenomenon in which an individual, typically a political leader or public figure, is elevated to an almost god-like status by their followers or supporters. It involves the creation of an intense and often blind adoration, where the person's image is carefully crafted and propagated to inspire unwavering loyalty and devotion. The individual in question utilizes various techniques to cultivate a larger-than-life persona, projecting an image of power, charisma, and infallibility. This image is meticulously constructed through propaganda, media control, and the shaping of narratives that promote the person's virtues while downplaying or suppressing any flaws or shortcomings. The cult of personality thrives on the emotional connection between the leader and their followers. It relies on the exploitation of human desires and aspirations, tapping into the hopes and dreams of the masses. By presenting themselves as saviors, visionaries or embodying certain ideologies, these figures offer their followers a sense of purpose and direction, promising a better future under their guidance.

Under General Augusto Pinochet's rule in Chile, a cult of personality was deliberately cultivated to promote and reinforce the belief in Pinochet's leadership and authority. The historical SPICE themes refer to five broad categories that are commonly used to analyze and understand the development and evolution of human societies over time. These themes are Social, Political, Interactions With Humans and the Environment, Culture, ​Economic. Scholars and researchers use these themes to organize their thinking and research, and to help identify the key factors that have influenced the development of societies.

  • Social : The social theme can be used to analyze how societies have structured social relationships, and how social structures have developed over time. This can include studying the roles and expectations of individuals based on factors such as gender, race, class, and other social categories, as well as the ways in which social structures have influenced political and economic systems.​ 
  • Political : ​The political theme can be used to analyze how societies have organized themselves politically, and how political systems have developed over time. This can include studying the development of different forms of government, the distribution of power, and the relationships between rulers and subjects.
  • Interactions With Humans and the Environment:  The environmental theme can be used to analyze how societies have interacted with their natural surroundings, and how environmental factors have influenced social and political structures. This can include studying the impact of climate, geography, and natural resources on the development of societies. 
  • Culture : The cultural theme can be used to analyze how societies have developed unique beliefs, values, customs, and practices, and how these cultural elements have influenced social and political structures. This can include studying the development of language, religion, art, music, literature, and social norms. 
  • Economic : The economic theme can be used to analyze how societies have organized their economies and how economic systems have developed over time. This can include studying the development of agriculture, trade, manufacturing, and finance, as well as the impact of economic systems on social and political structures.
